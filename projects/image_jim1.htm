Signal Processing for Enhanced Pipeline Inspection
Jim Hare (hare@reverb.crl.mcmaster.ca)

Project Overview

This project has to do with non-destructive testing of installed pipelines. Oil and gas carried by the pipelines can contain relatively high levels of sulphur, which is corrosive to the steel pipe. If this corrosion is left unchecked, it may result in rupture of the pipeline. The ensuing environmental damage would be catastrophic. Pipelines must therefore be closely monitored and inspected to ensure that any corrosion or defects in the pipe wall have not affected the integrity of the pipe. The preferred method of inspection is to collect data using an autonomous device referred to as a "pig" or inspection tool, that is propelled down the pipeline by the natural flow of oil or gas transport. This device, after inducing a magnetic field to flow through the pipe wall, measures flux leakage using Hall effect sensors, which are located all around the circumference of the pig. When the sensors travel by an area where there is an anomaly of some kind in the pipe wall, there is a resulting fringing effect in the flux leakages that leads to a change in output level from the sensors. The sensor outputs are stored on-board the pig. The data gathered in this way constitute an image of the interior of the entire pipe. The business of the industrial sponsor, Pipetronix Ltd., is to provide such a pipeline inspection service to its clients.

Relevant points regarding this imaging process
  • Potentially harmful defects caused by corrosion in the interior pipe surface are easily detected by this magnetic technique. The detection of the defects themselves, and the determination of the size and shape of these defects are the primary reasons why the pipeline is inspected, and hence are the items of critical concern to the client.
  • A direct technique for fabricating pipelines is to bend steel sheets into cylinders (referred to as "long seam" pipes). The cylinders are then butt-welded (to form "girth welds") together on-site. The images of the resulting long seam and girth welds, which constitute a completed pipeline section, are items of interest to Pipetronix and its clients.
  • Another more sophisticated technique for the fabrication of pipe is to force a mandrill down the centre of a red-hot cylindrical section of steel. The pipe is formed after several passes with successively larger mandrills. This type of pipe is referred to as "seamless". This process results in pipe which is scored on the inside. This scoring results in a significant level of background "noise" in the magnetic image, and can obscure significant defects or other areas of concern.
  • A pipeline usually contains flanges, fittings, valves, etc. , of various sorts. The identification and location of these items is of interest to the client.
  • Currently, a simple image compression algorithm is used in storing the data on-board the pig. This algorithm introduces occasional anomalies into the reconstructed image, referred to as "reset noise". It is important to identify this noise as a distinct class of object so that it is not confused with an actual item of interest.
  • The magnetic sensors have a point-spread function (PSF) of significant width, in both dimensions. This PSF is not known a priori, since it changes over varying conditions encountered on a run. The effect of this PSF is to blur and degrade the image quality.
Typical Images

A typical magnetic image of a section of long seam pipe is shown in Fig. 1. The vertical axis is the circumferential direction of the pipe (sensor index), whereas the horizontal axis is along the axial direction of the pipe (time-sample index). Colour represents magnetic intensity, going from red (least metal) to blue (most metal). The letters "D", "R", and "W" represent regions of defect, reset noise, and girth weld, respectively.

A typical magnetic image of a section of seamless pipe is shown in Fig. 2. The background noise characteristic due to the scoring of the pipe during fabrication is clearly evident. In view of the fact many of the red areas are NOT defects, it is also evident from the figure how defects (marked as before with a "D") can be obscured by the noise. We note that the noise characteristic is far from white in both spatial dimensions. This fact enables gains to be made in the detection of defects by using intelligent signal processing schemes.
The Problem

Currently, the stored data collected from the pig is analyzed by human operator, to identify regions of defect, weld types, etc. The intent of this co-operative research and development (CRD) project is to develop methods for streamlining and automating this analysis process. This poses many interesting and challenging signal and image processing problems including:

  • The classification of installation type: seamless or non-seamless.
  • The classification and detection of all objects of interest: defects, welds, areas of metal gain and metal loss, reset noise, flanges, etc. , into their respective classes.
  • The detection of defects that are masked by the background noise in seamless pipe.
  • The compression of data: The quantity of data generated by the magnetic scanning process is huge. Therefore, there is a demand for state-of-the-art image compression techniques which can reduce the quantity of data which must be stored on-board the pig to a minimum, while still preserving the best possible reconstructed image quality.
  • The deconvolution of the image with the point spread function (PSF) of the sensors, to produce an image of enhanced resolution. This will improve classification performance and detectability of defects, and will also aid in the assessment of the severity of defects. Since the PSF is unknown and cannot be reliably determined by conventional means, the deconvolution must be done blindly.
